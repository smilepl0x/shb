---
title: Old man yells at cloud (AI)
tags: ["AI", "tools", "humanity"]
---

AI is a complicated subject for me. On the one hand, I try to stay away from it.

That's because: _I like to code_.

I started coding as a kid and I very much so enjoy spending gobs of my time
solving hard problems. Even ones that have been solved already. My inclination
is that my brain is muscle and if I don't use it, I'll lose it. I'm not a doctor
and this is just my feeling, but it's a strong one, especially with the newly
termed [digital dementia](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10353947/)
showing up.

But, on the other hand, even as someone who feels as if they have a good handle
on the work that flows (when I'm lucky) from my fingertips, I'm finding AI
harder and harder to get away from. There is an AI alternative product for every
service out there. AI writers, AI readers, AI companions, AI milkshakes. Kidding
on that last one, but you get the idea. Even for a simple Google query, "AI
Mode" displays prominently at the top of the page with its quick stream of
letters, forming sentences faster than I can read them.

And, you know what? Much of the time, I find those answers completely
appropriate and helpful. It saves me time trudging through StackOverflow posts
looking for someone that was courageous enough to display their ignorance in a
public forum in the hopes that some kind Senior Engineer would drop in and bless
them.

Of course, this makes me wonder: how much time was I spending looking through SO
for answers instead of coding? Did AI actually save me time? Is my frustration
with it ill-placed? Is this just Turbo-Google? Probably yes, in a lot of ways.

But I, like probably many others, feel like the line of responsible AI use is
blurring. For some decades now, we have been in the age of "ask and you shall
recieve" that can only be brought to you by the incredible power of the
internet. The kneejerk reaction is to reject this quicker than a kid with a big
plate of peas because I feel like figuring things out for yourself is an
important quality in a person, but there is no doubting the force multiplier
that AI brings to the production of... well, a whole lot of stuff. Oh god, was
there ever any difference between asking SO and using an AI tool?

And so, somewhere between the wonders of connectivity that we got in the 90s and
the blatant disregard for privacy that came in with just about everything after
that ([not just social media!](https://www.sinceyouarrived.world/taken)), we
have finally reached a point where we can zoom out a little bit and separate the
good from the bad. We should be asking ourselves the hard questions, like, "Is
this worth my privacy?". The answer to which, only you can answer. Call me
paranoid, but my answer to that is a resounding "Hell no!". Privacy and
ownership is a very important right and I strongly feel that we should resist
any forces that try to take that away from us, overtly or subvertly.

You may not agree with any of this. You may have some very valid use cases for
integrating AI into your daily life (I'd love to hear about them!). You may feel
coerced into using it in order to keep your job (_raises hand_). I'm not any
sort of medical or technological authority on any of this stuff, but I do know
that money is God. And I'm highly skeptical of any approach that doesn't answer
or just ignores any questions about how this technology affects human
sovereignty, or even health, while raking in millions of dollars at the
forefront of all of our consciousnesses.

Just some things to think about. Don't let me rain on your parade. :)

This post was not written using AI tools. Obviously.
